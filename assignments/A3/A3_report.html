Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • We can see that the data starts in March 2020 and ends in early January 2022 and the cases are the 7-day average of the data.
  • The thickness specified in the "legend" is from 0 to 150k cases, which is an interesting number to choose since we can see parts where the scale is way bigger than 150k cases.
  • We can see the spikes in cases is usually during the winter, my theory being since everyone spends time inside because of the cold.

Design Critique:

  • The width specified in the "legend" is from 0 to 150k cases, for everything else you would have to take an educated guess or use a ruler to measure the distance and then do math to get an estimate. This is not a good technique since it may lead to misinformation and is simply just making the life of the reader harder since they need to do more work.
  • Having the center of the circle not be in the middle of the visualization might not help convince the reader that this is a real visualization made with real data. Think about it as a magic trick, usually the ones that are more sophisticated work better than other that do not look well executed.
  • Another problem I can see is that as the years pass, the size of each month in the visualization gets bigger and bigger making visual comparisons between years more difficult. People might think that since it is bigger it means there are more cases... or other scenarios.
  • A friend of mine saw this visualization on my computer and their first reaction was: "what is that and why does it look bad?". This is a good example that shows that people are not likely to understand the visualization completely.
  • I believe more text should be added to the visualization that explains what is going in the image and how to understand completely the visualization.
  • The beginning of the spiral is also not even painted with the red/pink they use since the data is 0 for that time and I think it shouldn't be used for the visualization.

Final Visualization Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

First, I added a column to the original data (which can be found here) which has the average of the last seven days of newly confirmed cases for each day in the data. I used Python (the pandas library) to make the calculation and add the column to the data and save the data to a new csv file which can be found here. The code for this process can be found here. I also did not use the last data point shown in the original data since it has a negative number of newly confirmed cases and the date is not continuous with the rest of the data. From the different visualizations I made on pen and paper, I felt the one that best expressed the data from the original visualization from the NYT was the Bubble Chart. My visualization attempts to answer the question "Which months have had the most newly confirmed cases on average?" while it still shows the same data from the NYT visualization. For the visualization, I used a different color for each month of the year, for any reader to see that in specific months of the year, the new cases went up consistently. I used a Bubble Chart since it shows the magnitude of each month in an easy-to-understand way. The size of the circles are the reflection of the number inside the circle which is the average of the new cases. One thing I tried adjusting, but it messed the order of the circles was the order of the months in the legend. That is one part I wish I could fix without changing the order of the bubbles.

Reflection

I had six main critiques when I made step 2 and those are: width being max 150k cases, center of the circle not being in the center, the months size in the NYT visualization was changing after each year, visualization appeal, original visualization having a bad title and legend, and finally, they added months that had data values being 0. I believe that the Bubble Chart effectively covers all my critiques by doing the following: not limiting the scale to be 150k new cases in average but allowing the maximum, it has the chart centered, all the months now change according to the average new cases for that month, bubble charts are fun and appealing to read, the title is descriptive, and the legend has all the months of the year (only bad thing about this is that they are not in order, I couldn't figure how to do it without changing the configuration of the bubbles), and I only included months in the chart that had nonzero positive data. Critique by redesign was for sure a helpful way to identify challenges of communicating the data in the NYT visualization. There were definitely tradeoffs for each of the visualization, but I believe that the Bubble Chart was the best one to interpret the data.
